The Competition

Technovation Girls

Technovation Girls is the world's largest technology entrepreneurship program for girls, challenging young women in over 100 countries to identify a real-world problem and build a mobile or web app to address it.

Founded in 2010, the programme has engaged more than 50,000 girls across six continents. Participants do not just learn to code; they learn to think like entrepreneurs, conducting user research, building business plans, and pitching their ideas to real-world judges from leading technology companies.

The 2026 competition focuses on pressing global issues including climate change, biodiversity loss, and environmental justice. Teams must demonstrate that their solutions are grounded in real research, built with real technology, and tested with real users.

Our team from Georgia entered this competition with one conviction: technology can make environmental action accessible to anyone, not just governments and large NGOs.

Why current solutions fall short

Our research revealed one recurring gap: people who want to help have no single platform where they can find planting opportunities, fund verified action, and safely report illegal logging.

Green Guard closes all three gaps in one transparent platform and turns concern into measurable action.

Green Guard

How Our Platform Works

Green Guard combines funding, volunteering, and citizen reporting in one platform with visible, map-based impact.

01
Interactive Live Map

Users see active planting sites, sponsored trees, and deforestation reports in one live view.

02
Tree Sponsorship

Sponsors can support one-time or recurring planting and track outcomes through mapped records.

03
Volunteer Network

Verified volunteers receive clear campaign guidance on site, species, and planting process.

04
Citizen Reporting

People can safely report illegal logging and environmental damage through a simple workflow.

05
Business Leaderboard

Companies are ranked by impact, encouraging transparent, measurable environmental investment.
